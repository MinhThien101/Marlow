# Framework: Educational Guide

Educational Guide teaches one thing that sharpens the product case. Use it when the reader needs missing context to understand value: a mechanism, misconception, best practice, or how-it-works lesson tied to a real feature. Use it in welcome flows after the offer, authority-building flow slots, and standalone POV campaigns. Do not use it when the offer is the campaign's reason to exist. Do not write a blog post in disguise. Playbook rule: teach simply, not like a textbook. Teach ONE thing. Every teaching beat must sharpen the product case; if it works the same without the product, it has drifted.

## Structural rules

- Hook: a question, a misconception, or a single concrete fact. Short. The reader should know what they are about to learn by the end of the first line.
- Teaching: 2 to 4 short beats. Concrete points, not abstract category claims. If the reader cannot picture it, rewrite.
- Connect to product: explicit. The lesson and the product share a single sentence that links them. "That is exactly why..." / "Which is what we built..." / "Which is what this product solves."
- CTA: one. Practical, not aspirational. Verb matches the lesson ("See how we built it", "Shop the routine", "Read the full breakdown").
- Optional close: one short line if the lesson invites a personal beat.
- Do not teach two lessons in one email. Pick the strongest and cut the rest.

## When the brief should call it

```
**Campaign Direction**: <one sentence naming the single lesson the email teaches and how it sharpens the product case>

**Product Focus**: <the product the lesson connects to>

**Links**: <verified destination, often a category or product page>

**Framework**: Educational Guide
```

The brief names the lesson. The copywriter does not invent studies, stats, or claims. Unverifiable claims become `missing info: <what is missing>` in the brief.

## Example structural fragment

```
Hey [FIRST_NAME],

<HOOK: one short question, misconception, or concrete fact>

<TEACHING BEAT 1: one short paragraph, one specific point>

<TEACHING BEAT 2: one short paragraph, building on beat 1>

<CONNECT TO PRODUCT: one explicit linking sentence>

[<CTA VERB PHRASE: matches the lesson>](<verified link from brief>)

[UNSUBSCRIBE_LINK]
```

If the email teaches obvious category 101, the angle is wrong. Pick a non-obvious lesson that directly sharpens the product case.
