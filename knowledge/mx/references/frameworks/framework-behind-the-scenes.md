# Framework: Behind The Scenes

Behind The Scenes turns process into credibility. Use it when the brand has real work to show that the customer can appreciate: a product development beat, a manufacturing step, a quality-control moment, a founder's design process, a sourcing trip, a team behind a recent launch. Use it in standalone campaigns where trust and authenticity are the lever, and in flow positions where the brand needs a non-sales-y human beat. Do not use it when the "behind the scenes" is invented or generic; readers can smell manufactured access. Do not turn it into an internal diary. Playbook rule: show work the customer can appreciate. Specific process details build trust. Do not ramble. The reader should leave with one concrete fact about how the brand actually operates.

## Structural rules

- Opening: personal. Founder voice common. "It is [FOUNDER_FIRST_NAME]" or a direct first-person line.
- Process setup: one short paragraph naming what the reader is about to see behind the curtain.
- What goes into the work: one or two short beats. Concrete details: hours, iterations, materials, sourcing, testing rounds, real decisions made.
- Why that matters to the reader: one short beat. Quality, durability, fit, ingredient sourcing, fair trade, real outcome.
- CTA: one. Optional if the email is sentiment-only. Verb matches ("See the collection", "Shop the result", "Read the full story"). Soft CTAs work well.
- Sign-off: optional. Founder voice common.
- Do not turn the email into a corporate diary ("here is what our team has been working on this quarter"). One specific process beat is stronger than a roundup.

## When the brief should call it

```
**Campaign Direction**: <one sentence naming the specific process moment and what it reveals>

**Product Focus**: <the product or collection the process points at>

**Links**: <verified destination, often the product or collection page>

**Framework**: Behind The Scenes
```

The brief carries the real process details. The copywriter does not invent a sourcing trip, a testing round, or a team member.

## Example structural fragment

```
Hey [FIRST_NAME],

<OPENING: founder voice, one short personal line>

<PROCESS SETUP: one short paragraph naming what is behind the curtain>

<WHAT GOES INTO THE WORK: one or two short beats with concrete details>

<WHY THAT MATTERS: one short beat tying the process to the reader>

[<OPTIONAL CTA>](<verified link from brief>)

[UNSUBSCRIBE_LINK]
```

If the email reads as a general "we work hard at quality" beat, the framework has drifted. Replace with the one specific process detail that only this brand could write.
