# Feature Highlight Section

Feature Highlight goes deep on ONE mechanism, ingredient, or design choice and turns it into a clear reason to believe. Use it when the product wins on a specific technical or design lever the reader needs to understand to choose it: a proprietary mechanism, a patented design, a hero ingredient, a distinctive build approach. Do not use it when several features matter equally; that is a Benefit List. Do not use it to dump every feature into one section; the discipline is going deep on ONE thing.

Counts as a HEAVY section. Best when the product genuinely needs explanation.

## Structural rules

- One feature. One. The whole section is about the named mechanism.
- Required: a heading that names the feature or the payoff (or both, as a stacked double-headline).
- Required: 1 short framing line stating the problem the feature solves or the claim it backs.
- Required: 2 to 4 numbered sub-points or short bolded items that break the feature into its working parts.
- Each sub-point: a bold label naming the part, then 1 to 2 short sentences explaining how it works and what it does for the reader.
- Required: one CTA pointing at the deeper science page, the product page, or the configurator.
- Concrete language wins. "Independent lower back system" beats "advanced support technology".
- Length: longer than a benefit list, shorter than a brand story. The reader should leave understanding the one mechanism.

## Placeholder shape in the brief

```
**Email Sections**:
1. Hero Section
2. Feature Highlight
3. <next canonical section label>

**Product Focus**: <the product, with the specific feature/mechanism named if the brand specified it>
**Links**: <verified science page, product page, or deeper explanation page>
```

The brief names the section and may name the specific feature to highlight. The technical detail comes from verified product facts and brand state, never from invention.

## Example structural fragment

```
## <PROBLEM HEADING: the friction the feature solves>

## <PAYOFF HEADING: what the feature does about it>

<ONE FRAMING LINE: the claim or the contrast with the default approach>

<NAMED CLAIM SENTENCE: one sentence introducing the proprietary mechanism, with its name>

**01. <SUB-PART LABEL>**
<ONE TO TWO SHORT SENTENCES on how this part works and what it gives the reader>

**02. <SUB-PART LABEL>**
<ONE TO TWO SHORT SENTENCES>

**03. <SUB-PART LABEL>**
<ONE TO TWO SHORT SENTENCES>

[<CTA VERB PHRASE: see the science, shop, learn more>](<verified link from brief>)
```

Three numbered sub-points is the working shape. Two work when the feature is genuinely two-part. Four is the ceiling; beyond that, the section is no longer one feature.
