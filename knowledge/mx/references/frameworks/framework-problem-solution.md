# Framework: Problem-Solution

Problem-Solution names a pain the reader recognizes, shows why the usual fix fails, and positions the product as the cleanest answer. Use it when the product solves a real, specific friction the audience already feels: a category that under-delivers, a workaround that breaks under load, a default option that is more expensive or less effective than the brand. Use it in welcome flows and standalone campaigns where the brand needs to stake out a clear position. Do not use it when the pain is abstract or invented; the reader will not recognize manufactured friction. Do not over-agitate. Playbook rule: pain is specific, not abstract. Do not stretch the agitation just to sound dramatic. The product answer should feel inevitable by the end, not announced.

## Structural rules

- Pain point: one short paragraph naming the friction in concrete terms. The reader should nod by the end of the second sentence.
- Why it matters: one beat tying the pain to a real consequence (time, money, comfort, results). Skip if the pain carries its own weight.
- Why common alternatives fail: one short beat. Name the default the reader probably tried. Not strawman; fair characterization.
- Solution reveal: explicit. The product, the mechanic, or the brand stance. One short paragraph.
- CTA: one. Verb matches the solution ("See how we built it", "Try the fix", "Adjust my routine").
- Sign-off: optional.
- Do not stack three pains. One named pain, one named alternative, one named solution.

## When the brief should call it

```
**Campaign Direction**: <one sentence naming the specific pain and how the product is the answer>

**Product Focus**: <the product the solution reveal points at>

**Links**: <verified destination>

**Framework**: Problem-Solution
```

The brief names the pain in concrete terms. The copywriter does not invent statistics about how widespread the pain is.

## Example structural fragment

```
Hey [FIRST_NAME],

<PAIN POINT: one short paragraph, specific and recognizable, two sentences max>

<WHY IT MATTERS: one beat on the real consequence, optional if pain carries it>

<WHY COMMON ALTERNATIVES FAIL: one short beat, fair not strawman>

<SOLUTION REVEAL: the product or mechanic, one short paragraph>

[<CTA VERB PHRASE: matches solution>](<verified link from brief>)

[UNSUBSCRIBE_LINK]
```

If the pain reads like marketing-blog generic ("Are you tired of feeling tired?"), the angle is wrong. Pick a friction the reader actually feels this week.
