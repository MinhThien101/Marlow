# Brand Story Section

Brand Story is the emotional trust block. It is grounded in a specific origin, mission, or founder moment that explains why this brand exists. Use it when the reader needs reason-to-trust before reason-to-buy: welcome flows, founder-led launches, mission-led products, or campaigns where the brand identity is the differentiator. Do not use it as filler in a transactional send. Do not use it when generic mission language is the only material available; if the story is not specific, the section is not earning its place. Counts as a HEAVY section; one brand story plus one other heavy block is the cap.

## Structural rules

- One narrative arc. Origin moment, founder decision, or mission turning point. Not three.
- Required: a section heading that signals the story's emotional anchor (not the brand name).
- Required: 2 to 4 short paragraphs telling the specific story. Concrete names, dates, places, decisions.
- Required: one CTA pointing at the broader brand narrative (about page, founder letter, story page) or at the product the story justifies.
- The emotional turn connects directly to a product reality. If the story does not change how the reader sees the product, it does not belong in this email.
- No generic mission statements. No "we believe" language without a grounded moment behind it.
- Length: longer than a benefit item, shorter than a blog post. The reader should finish in under 30 seconds.

## Placeholder shape in the brief

```
**Email Sections**:
1. Hero Section
2. Brand Story
3. <next canonical section label>

**Links**: <about page, story page, or founder letter URL>
```

The brief names the section and provides the link the CTA points at. The story content comes from brand state (brand profile, memories, recent campaigns), not from the brief.

## Example structural fragment

```
## <HEADING: emotional anchor, not the brand name>

<PARAGRAPH 1: the specific moment or decision that started the brand>

<PARAGRAPH 2: the realization or turning point, with concrete detail>

<OPTIONAL PARAGRAPH 3: what changed as a result>

<OPTIONAL CLOSING LINE: the through-line connecting story to product>

[<CTA VERB PHRASE: read, see, learn>](<verified story or product link from brief>)
```

Three short paragraphs is the working size. If you are writing a fourth, ask whether the second paragraph is doing real work or just restating the setup.
