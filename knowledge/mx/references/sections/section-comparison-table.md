# Comparison Table Section

A Comparison Table is a side-by-side contrast that helps the reader justify the choice. Use it when the differentiation is the actual decision driver: this product vs a category default, this version vs an alternative the reader is weighing, this brand's approach vs the standard approach. Use it in educational campaigns and welcome flows where the reader does not yet know why this option is the right one. Do not use it as a brag list (this brand vs "the competition" with strawman columns); a comparison that is not fair is the kind of move that erodes trust. Do not use it for products the reader has no alternative for; there is nothing to compare.

Counts as a HEAVY section.

## Structural rules

- 2 to 3 columns. Two is the standard (this vs that). Three works when there is a clear third option in the market.
- 3 to 6 rows. Below 3 is not enough to justify a table; above 6 stops scanning.
- Each row covers one comparison dimension (intensity, price tier, ingredient, use case, outcome). One dimension per row.
- Comparisons are concrete and fair. If the row would not hold up to a buyer's friend pointing at it, it does not belong.
- Headers are the option names, not promotional labels. "DELTA-9 THC" beats "The Heavy Stuff".
- Bold the column headers and the row labels. The body cells stay plain.
- Optional: a short heading above the table framing the comparison.
- Optional: a CTA below pointing at the option the brand sells.

## Placeholder shape in the brief

```
**Email Sections**:
1. Hero Section
2. Comparison Table
3. <next canonical section label>

**Product Focus**: <the product or category being compared>
**Client Specifications / Additional Notes**: <the comparison dimensions the brand cares about, if specified>
```

The brief names the section and the focus. The copywriter pulls the comparison dimensions and the verified facts for each cell from brand state and product data, not from imagination.

## Example structural fragment

```
## <OPTIONAL HEADING: e.g. "<OPTION A> VS. <OPTION B>">

|   | **<OPTION A NAME>** | **<OPTION B NAME>** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **<DIMENSION 1>** | <CONCRETE FACT> | <CONCRETE FACT> |
| **<DIMENSION 2>** | <CONCRETE FACT> | <CONCRETE FACT> |
| **<DIMENSION 3>** | <CONCRETE FACT> | <CONCRETE FACT> |
| **<DIMENSION 4>** | <CONCRETE FACT> | <CONCRETE FACT> |

[<OPTIONAL CTA VERB PHRASE>](<verified link from brief>)
```

Four to five rows is the working range. If the comparison is forcing rows that read as filler, drop them and let the table breathe; a tight three-row table outperforms a padded six-row one.
