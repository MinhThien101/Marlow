# Hero Section

The hero is the above-the-fold opener. It lands the campaign's main promise or offer in one beat and gets out of the way for whatever comes next. Use it in every DESIGNED email; hero is always first and never optional. Do not use the hero to set context, introduce the brand, or warm the reader up. If the reader needs setup before they can understand the headline, the campaign angle is wrong, not the hero.

## Structural rules

- Required: one short headline that names the main promise or offer.
- Optional: one short support line if the headline cannot stand alone.
- Required: one CTA pointing at the same objective as the headline.
- Order: headline, optional support line, CTA. Nothing else.
- Length: headline is 4 to 8 words. Support line is one sentence at most. Skip the support line when the headline carries the load alone.
- The headline and the CTA name the same job. If they disagree, the reader leaves.
- No body paragraphs. No multi-line setup. No secondary CTAs. No section labels.

## Placeholder shape in the brief

```
## <campaign title>

**Email Sections**:
1. Hero Section
2. <next canonical section label>
```

The brief names the section. It does not pre-write the headline or hint at copy. The copywriter picks the words from the Campaign Direction and Product Focus.

## Example structural fragment

```
# <HERO HEADLINE: 4-8 words naming the main promise or offer>

<OPTIONAL SUPPORT LINE: one sentence, only if the headline cannot stand alone>

[<CTA VERB PHRASE: matches headline objective>](<verified link from brief>)
```

That is the entire hero. If a fourth line is creeping in, the section has drifted out of hero shape.
