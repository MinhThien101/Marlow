# Lifestyle Content Section

Lifestyle Content places the product inside a scene, ritual, identity, or cultural moment so the reader can picture using it. Use it when the product is a lifestyle decision as much as a utility purchase: apparel, beauty, beverages, accessories, anything where context drives the buy. Use it when the brand has a real cultural anchor (a community, an aesthetic, a usage occasion) to draw from. Do not use it as generic mood-setting; if the section could fit any brand, it does not belong to this one. Lifestyle reads as "soft" but counts as a HEAVY section in the structural weight model; it does real persuasive work and takes real word count.

## Structural rules

- Anchor in one scenario, identity, or cultural cue. Not a list of three vibes.
- Required: a section heading that names the moment or the identity, not the product name.
- Required: 2 to 4 short paragraphs of editorial-feeling prose, or a structured "ways to wear / ways to use" sub-block.
- Required: one CTA pointing at the product, collection, or page that fits the lifestyle frame.
- Specifics make it lived-in. Named occasions, named places, named pairings, named outfits. Generic adjectives ("perfect for any occasion") flatten it.
- The product appears as part of the scene, not the subject of the scene. The reader is the subject; the product is the way the reader shows up.
- Length: longer than a benefit list, shorter than a brand story. Editorial pacing, short sentences.

## Placeholder shape in the brief

```
**Email Sections**:
1. Hero Section
2. Lifestyle Content
3. <next canonical section label>

**Product Focus**: <the product or collection the lifestyle frame supports>
**Client Specifications / Additional Notes**: <the scenario, identity, or cultural cue the brand wants used, if specified>
```

The brief names the section and the focus product. The scenario comes from brand state (positioning, audience identity, recent campaigns), not from generic lifestyle stock.

## Example structural fragment

```
## <HEADING: names the scenario, identity, or cultural cue>

<PARAGRAPH 1: place the reader inside the scene with concrete detail>

<PARAGRAPH 2: the product's role inside that scene, not as the subject>

<OPTIONAL "WAYS TO" SUB-BLOCK>

### 01. <SCENARIO LABEL>
**The Setting:** <one concrete line>
**The Kit:** <one concrete line: product plus its real-world pairings>
**Why it Works:** <one concrete line tying the choice to the identity>

### 02. <SCENARIO LABEL>
**The Setting:** <one concrete line>
**The Kit:** <one concrete line>
**Why it Works:** <one concrete line>

[<CTA VERB PHRASE: shop, build the kit, see the collection>](<verified link from brief>)
```

The "Ways To" sub-block is the structured variant for occasion-driven products. Plain paragraphs are the editorial variant for identity-driven products. Both work; pick the one that fits the campaign.
