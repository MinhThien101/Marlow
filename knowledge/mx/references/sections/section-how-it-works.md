# How It Works Section

How It Works is a numbered-step block that demystifies the buying or using process. Use it when the product has a non-obvious flow (impression kits, subscriptions, configurators, multi-step services) and uncertainty is a real conversion blocker. Use it in welcome flows after the hero offer when the reader is new to the model. Do not use it for products the reader already understands; explaining "add to cart, check out" wastes the section. Counts as a HEAVY section.

## Structural rules

- 3 to 5 numbered steps. Below 3 is not a sequence; above 5 means the process is too complex for an email section.
- One job per step. If a step lists two actions, split it or cut the second action.
- Each step: a short bold label and 1 to 2 short sentences describing what happens.
- Steps are literal and sequential. No skipped beats, no implied prerequisites.
- Optional short heading above the steps. Optional one-line setup. No setup paragraph.
- Required: one CTA at the bottom pointing at the first step (start, order, begin) or at a deeper how-it-works page.
- The sequence is the persuasion. Generic adjectives ("easy", "simple", "fast") inside steps add nothing; the structure already says that.

## Placeholder shape in the brief

```
**Email Sections**:
1. Hero Section
2. How It Works
3. <next canonical section label>

**Product Focus**: <the product, service, or program whose process is being explained>
**Links**: <verified page where the reader starts the process>
```

The brief names the section and the destination link. It does not pre-write the steps; those come from brand state and verified product facts.

## Example structural fragment

```
## <HEADING: names the outcome or count, e.g. "4 SIMPLE STEPS TO X">

**1. <STEP LABEL>**
<ONE TO TWO SHORT SENTENCES describing what happens in this step>

**2. <STEP LABEL>**
<ONE TO TWO SHORT SENTENCES>

**3. <STEP LABEL>**
<ONE TO TWO SHORT SENTENCES>

**4. <STEP LABEL>**
<ONE TO TWO SHORT SENTENCES>

[<CTA VERB PHRASE: start, order, begin>](<verified link from brief>)
```

Four steps is the most common shape. Three is fine for very simple products. Five works when each step is genuinely distinct; if a fifth step is "we ship it" plus "you receive it" split in two, collapse them.
