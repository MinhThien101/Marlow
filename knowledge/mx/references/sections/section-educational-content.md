# Educational Content Section

Educational Content teaches one concrete lesson the reader can use or understand, and lets that lesson make the product more credible. Use it when the product depends on a fact the reader does not yet know: a niche category, a hidden problem, a misunderstood ingredient, a frequently-confused tradeoff. Use it in welcome flows after the offer, in problem-aware campaigns, and in post-purchase emails that teach how to get more out of the product. Do not use it for facts the reader already knows. Do not let it drift into general-interest territory; this is an email section, not a blog post. Teach one thing and stop.

Counts as a HEAVY section.

## Structural rules

- One lesson per section. Not three. If the brief implies three lessons, the email needs three sends, not three subsections.
- Required: a heading that names the lesson or the surprising fact, not the product category.
- Required: 1 to 3 short paragraphs or a short bulleted list that delivers the lesson.
- Required: a CTA that ties the lesson to the next action (shop the product, read the deeper guide, start the routine).
- The lesson connects back to the product or offer. A lesson the reader cannot act on is filler.
- Use concrete specifics (numbers, named parts, named ingredients). Generic "did you know" framing has no payoff.
- Length: under what a reasonable reader scans in 20 to 30 seconds. If the section starts sounding like an article, it is too long.

## Placeholder shape in the brief

```
**Email Sections**:
1. Hero Section
2. Educational Content
3. <next canonical section label>

**Product Focus**: <the product or routine the lesson supports>
**Client Specifications / Additional Notes**: <the specific lesson the brand wants taught, if specified>
```

The brief names the section and may name the lesson. The lesson content comes from brand state, verified product facts, and brand-owned research, never from invention.

## Example structural fragment

```
## <HEADING: names the lesson or the surprising fact>

<PARAGRAPH 1: the lesson itself, with one concrete specific>

<OPTIONAL PARAGRAPH 2 OR BULLET LIST: the supporting detail that makes the lesson useful>

<OPTIONAL CLOSING LINE: the tie back to product or next action>

[<CTA VERB PHRASE: shop, learn, start, read>](<verified link from brief>)
```

A short bulleted variant works when the lesson is "here are three ways to do X" or "here are the spots most people miss". A short paragraph variant works when the lesson is a single insight. Pick one shape and stay inside it.
