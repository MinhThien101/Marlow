# Framework: Story Arc

Story Arc pulls the reader through a beginning, middle, and reveal. Use it when the brand has a real story that earns the product or mission: founder origin, customer transformation (with permission), discovery story, or turning point that reframes the category. Use it sparingly; overuse weakens impact. It works best in welcome-flow story slots and standalone campaigns that need narrative weight. Do not use it without a real story. Do not stretch thin anecdotes. Playbook rule: keep the arc human, not cinematic for its own sake. Every turn must move the argument forward.

## Structural rules

- Story opening: one short paragraph. Set the scene with one specific detail. Not "once upon a time" framing.
- Conflict or discovery: one short paragraph. What went wrong, what got found, what the protagonist did not know yet.
- Turning point: one short paragraph. The decision, the realization, the moment that changed the trajectory.
- Meaning and product tie-in: one short paragraph. What the story means for the brand and the reader. The product or standard the story produced.
- CTA: one. Soft when the story is sentiment-led, direct when the story leads into a product. Verb matches the story's outcome ("See our standards", "Shop the result", "Read the full story").
- Sign-off: optional. Founder voice common.
- Do not pad. Four short paragraphs beats six long ones.

## When the brief should call it

```
**Campaign Direction**: <one sentence naming the story and the meaning it produces>

**Product Focus**: <the product, standard, or page the story leads to>

**Required Language**: <exact names, dates, or specifics if the user provided them>

**Links**: <verified destination>

**Framework**: Story Arc
```

The brief must carry real story specifics. Invented names, dates, or events become `missing info: <what is missing>` in the brief.

## Example structural fragment

```
Hey [FIRST_NAME],

<OPENING: one short paragraph with one specific scene detail>

<CONFLICT OR DISCOVERY: one short paragraph naming the friction or the find>

<TURNING POINT: one short paragraph on the decision or realization>

<MEANING AND PRODUCT TIE-IN: one short paragraph connecting the story to the brand>

[<CTA VERB PHRASE>](<verified link from brief>)

[UNSUBSCRIBE_LINK]
```

If the story becomes cinematic ramble with no clear meaning, it has drifted. Every turn must move toward the brand or product.
