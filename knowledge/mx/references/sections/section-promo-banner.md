# Promo Banner Section

A Promo Banner is a short supporting strip near the top of the email, before or alongside the hero, that carries a single piece of context: an offer reminder, a quick urgency line, or a contest hook. It is not the main body of the email; if it becomes the main body, it should have been Discount Offer or the hero itself. Use it when the email has a real first message (hero, story, education) and the offer needs to be visible above the fold without taking over. Do not use it as a second hero. Do not stack it with an Urgency Banner; one offer-context strip per email.

Counts as a SHORT / scan-first section. Often the 4th section in a 4-section DESIGNED email where the other three include heavy blocks.

## Structural rules

- Required: one message. One offer, or one urgency note, or one promo line. Not two.
- Required: one CTA if the message is a clickable offer. Optional CTA if the banner just sets context for the hero below.
- Optional: a short heading framing the message.
- Length: 1 to 3 short lines including the CTA. If a fourth line is creeping in, the section is becoming the email.
- Promo Banner near the top vs Discount Offer mid-email: pick one home for the offer, not both.

## Placeholder shape in the brief

```
**Email Sections**:
1. Promo Banner
2. Hero Section
3. <next canonical section label>

**Offer**: <discount mechanic, code, expiration. Or "No offer".>
**Links**: <verified link the CTA points at, if the banner has its own CTA>
```

When the brief places the Promo Banner before the Hero Section, the banner sets context the hero builds on. When it sits after the hero, it is a quick mid-email reminder strip.

## Example structural fragment

```
# <SHORT HEADING: 3-7 words naming the moment or hook>

<ONE LINE: the offer, the urgency cue, or the promo context. With code in **bold** if there is one.>

[<CTA VERB PHRASE>](<verified link from brief>)
```

The whole section should fit in three lines. If it does not, the campaign needs a different section type, not a longer banner.
