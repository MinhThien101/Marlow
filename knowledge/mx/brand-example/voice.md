# Voice: Spruce Coffee Co.

## Overview

Warm, plainspoken, and knowledgeable without being pedantic. Mara writes like
she is explaining something to a friend who asked a genuine question. She does
not simplify to the point of being condescending, but she also does not gate-
keep behind jargon.

The voice earns trust through specificity, not superlatives.


## Rule 1: Specificity over superlatives

Name the thing. Skip the adjective.

- Do: "The Oaxaca lot came in 200 lbs lighter than we ordered. We shipped it
  anyway."
- Avoid: "Incredible new beans, just arrived!"

Superlatives are easy to ignore. A specific fact is harder to dismiss.


## Rule 2: Calm confidence, not hype

The brand does not shout. Mara is confident in the product and does not need
to convince anyone by volume.

- Do: "If you have been drinking the Morning Standard for a while, the Huila
  might be worth a try. Different cup, same care."
- Avoid: "You NEED to try our new single-origin! Limited supply, order NOW!"

No all-caps. One exclamation point maximum per email, in the subject or preview
text only.


## Rule 3: Jargon is audience-dependent

For new buyers: avoid specialty coffee terminology unless it is explained in
the same sentence. Assume they do not know what "cupping notes" means.

For subscribers: use terminology freely. They have self-selected into deeper
engagement. "Anaerobic process" or "washed Ethiopian" needs no footnote in a
subscriber email.

Context signals: welcome series = new buyer. Subscription update = subscriber.
Reactivation = treat like new buyer.


## Rule 4: Mara's voice is present but not constant

Mara writes or signs most campaign emails. She does not sign every flow email.
Use first-person singular ("I roasted this batch...") only in emails Mara
explicitly signs. Otherwise, write in brand-voice first-person plural
("we roasted...") or second-person ("your order").

- Do: "We ran out of the Guatemala last Tuesday. More coming in April." (brand)
- Do: "I burned 14 pounds before I got the first roast right." (Mara signing)
- Avoid: mixing "I" and "we" in the same email without a clear speaker.


## Rule 5: Honest about limitations

Spruce does not over-promise. If stock is low, say so. If a roast is not for
everyone, say so.

- Do: "This one is for people who like a bright, acidic cup. If you are a dark
  roast drinker, the Morning Standard is a better fit."
- Avoid: "This coffee is for everyone! Whatever your taste, you will love it."

Honest constraints build trust. They also reduce returns and complaints.


## Rule 6: No forced urgency

Do not manufacture scarcity or deadlines unless they are real.

- Do: "The Huila lot is 80 lbs. When it is gone, it is gone until next season."
- Avoid: "Only a few bags left! Order before midnight!" (when stock is not
  actually limited)

Fake urgency trains readers to ignore it. Use it only when accurate.


## Rule 7: Short is default

Emails from Spruce run 100-200 words for promotional sends, 200-400 for
editorial or founder letters. Flows run shorter: 80-150 words for transactional
and onboarding steps.

If a sentence does not add information the reader needs to decide or act, cut it.
