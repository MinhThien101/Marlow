# Social Proof Section

Social Proof is a trust block built from reviews, testimonials, ratings, or buyer language. Use it after the email makes its core claim, when the brand should step aside and let real customers speak. Use it in browse-abandon and cart-abandon flows after product context and objection handling, where doubt is the blocker. Do not lead with proof; it will feel disconnected. Do not use unapproved or invented quotes; if neither brief nor brand state has them, mark `missing info: <what is missing>` and stop. Counts as a HEAVY section.

## Structural rules

- 2 to 4 testimonials. Fewer than 2 is anecdotal; more than 4 stops feeling curated.
- Each testimonial: a short quote in quotation marks, then an attribution line (first name plus initial, plus "Verified Buyer" or equivalent the brand actually uses).
- Optional: a short bolded summary line above each quote that names the angle ("Finally, peace of mind").
- Optional: section heading framing the proof.
- Quotes are specific. Generic "I love it" testimonials add nothing; the strong proof names the situation, the alternative tried, or the concrete outcome.
- Proof must be approved or user-provided. Brand state should carry the source.
- Place this section AFTER the core message and product context, never before. The reader needs to know what the proof supports.
- Optional CTA at the bottom pointing at the proof page (reviews page) or back at the product.

## Placeholder shape in the brief

```
**Email Sections**:
1. Hero Section
2. <core message section>
3. Social Proof

**Required Language**: <if the brief carries specific testimonial quotes the copy must use, list them here>
```

The brief flags required testimonials and provides exact quotes to use word-for-word. If the brief has no quotes, pull from approved brand state. If neither has them, mark the section as `missing info`, not invented copy.

## Example structural fragment

```
## <OPTIONAL HEADING: 3-6 words framing the proof>

<OPTIONAL ANGLE LABEL: e.g. **"Finally, the chair I needed"**>
"<EXACT APPROVED QUOTE FROM BRAND STATE OR BRIEF>"
- <Reviewer first name and last initial>, <Verified Buyer or equivalent>

<OPTIONAL ANGLE LABEL: **"<short angle>"**>
"<EXACT APPROVED QUOTE>"
- <Reviewer name>, <attribution>

[<OPTIONAL CTA VERB PHRASE>](<verified link from brief>)
```

Use angle-label-plus-quote when quotes do distinct jobs (different objections or use cases). Use plain quote-plus-attribution when they share one angle. Either is fine; pick one pattern and stay consistent.
