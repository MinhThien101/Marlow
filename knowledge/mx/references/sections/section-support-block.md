# Support Block Section

Support Block is the reassurance section: guarantees, returns, warranty terms, support availability, trial periods, and shipping protections. Use it when the product is a meaningful spend, online purchase risk is high (mattresses, chairs, electronics), shipping cost blocks conversion, or checkout-stage trust signals need emphasis. Place it late in the email or with checkout trust signals. Do not use it as filler; reassurance must resolve real friction.

Counts as a HEAVY section.

## Structural rules

- Required: a heading framing the reassurance ("YOUR COMFORT IS GUARANTEED", "TRY IT RISK-FREE").
- Required: 2 to 4 concrete reassurances, each with a bolded sub-label and a 1 to 2 sentence explanation.
- Each reassurance is specific: a named window (60-day, 12-year), a named scope (full refund, free replacement, US-based support), a named mechanism (the "Sit Test", the "Free Paint Guarantee").
- Vague reassurance is worse than none. "Great support" earns no trust; "12-year warranty, made in the USA" does.
- Required: one CTA pointing at the action the reassurance unblocks (start the trial, shop the product, contact support).
- Optional: a short setup line under the heading framing the risk this section dissolves.
- Do not invent guarantee terms. If the brief does not name trial window or warranty length, mark `missing info` in the brief and leave this section out.

## Placeholder shape in the brief

```
**Email Sections**:
1. Hero Section
2. <heavy section>
3. Support Block

**Client Specifications / Additional Notes**: <the specific guarantee terms, warranty length, return window, or support details>
**Links**: <link to the trial, return policy, or support page>
```

The brief carries guarantee facts verbatim. The copywriter never invents trial windows, warranty terms, refund scope, or support availability.

## Example structural fragment

```
## <HEADING: frames the reassurance, e.g. "YOUR COMFORT IS GUARANTEED">

<OPTIONAL SETUP LINE: one sentence on the risk this section dissolves>

**<REASSURANCE 1 LABEL: e.g. "The 60-Day Sit Test">**
<ONE TO TWO SENTENCES with the specific window, scope, and what the reader does to claim it>

**<REASSURANCE 2 LABEL: e.g. "12-Year Warranty">**
<ONE TO TWO SENTENCES with the specific term and what it covers>

**<OPTIONAL REASSURANCE 3 LABEL>**
<ONE TO TWO SENTENCES>

[<CTA VERB PHRASE: start the trial, shop risk-free, contact support>](<verified link from brief>)
```

Two reassurances is the floor. Three is most common. Four works when trust pillars are genuinely distinct. Beyond four, the section feels defensive.
