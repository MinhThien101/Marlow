# Urgency Banner Section

An Urgency Banner is a concentrated urgency moment placed lower in the email, after the main message has landed. Use it when a real urgency signal exists: low stock, last day of a sale, batch about to sell out, seasonal cutoff. Use it as the closer-stage push that nudges a reader who has read the rest. Do not use it without a real signal; manufactured urgency reads as a tell and damages trust on the next send. Do not stack it with a Promo Banner at the top of the same email; one urgency moment per send.

Counts as a SHORT / scan-first section. Best near the end of the email, not the beginning.

## Structural rules

- Required: an urgency hook line that names the specific signal (low stock, last day, batch ending, restock window).
- Required: a one-line consequence line that names what happens if the reader waits.
- Required: one CTA pointing at the urgent action.
- Optional: a short label above the hook (e.g. "LOW STOCK ALERT", "FINAL HOURS") to flag urgency before the headline lands.
- Length: short. Two short paragraphs at most. Long urgency banners stop feeling urgent.
- Concrete signal beats vague urgency. "Once these bags are gone, they don't come back until next year" beats "going fast".
- No invented urgency. If the brief did not name a real signal, the section does not belong in this send.

## Placeholder shape in the brief

```
**Email Sections**:
1. Hero Section
2. <heavy section>
3. Urgency Banner

**Offer**: <if urgency depends on the offer's expiration, that expiration belongs here>
**Client Specifications / Additional Notes**: <the real urgency signal: stock level, cutoff date, batch detail>
```

The brief carries the urgency fact. The section never invents one.

## Example structural fragment

```
## <OPTIONAL URGENCY LABEL: e.g. "LOW STOCK ALERT", "FINAL HOURS">

## <URGENCY HOOK: 4-8 words naming the specific signal>

<CONSEQUENCE LINE: one sentence on what happens if the reader waits, with concrete detail from the brief>

<OPTIONAL ACTION LINE: one sentence telling the reader what to do now>

[<CTA VERB PHRASE: shop, claim, secure, lock in>](<verified link from brief>)
```

The label-plus-headline double stack ("LOW STOCK ALERT" then the headline) is the standard urgency shape. Skip the label when the headline is strong enough alone.
